USS Nimitz Encounter

The Tic Tac Incident
November 14, 2004 · Pacific Ocean, ~100mi SW of San Diego · ~5 min visual encounter
Investigating

Executive Summary

During routine pre-deployment training exercises in the Pacific Ocean off Southern California, the USS Nimitz Carrier Strike Group encountered multiple anomalous aerial vehicles over approximately five days. The primary encounter on November 14, 2004 involved visual contact by two F/A-18F Super Hornet crews, radar tracking by the AN/SPY-1B system aboard USS Princeton, and infrared video capture via the ATFLIR pod.

The object — described as a white, featureless, elongated shape approximately 40 feet long resembling a Tic Tac candy — demonstrated flight characteristics far beyond any known technology, including instantaneous acceleration, hypersonic speed without sonic boom, and apparent awareness of intercepting aircraft.

The Encounter
Late October 2004
Anomalous radar contacts begin
USS Princeton's AN/SPY-1B radar detects objects at 80,000+ ft, descending to sea level in seconds — rates exceeding ballistic missile profiles. Senior Chief Kevin Day tracks returns over two weeks.
SPY-1Radar
November 14, 2004 · 1:00 PM
Fravor & Dietrich vectored to contact
CDR David Fravor and LCDR Alex Dietrich, flying two F/A-18F Super Hornets from VFA-41 "Black Aces," are diverted to a live radar return ~100 miles SW of San Diego.
FravorDietrich
1:10 PM · The Intercept
Visual contact — Tic Tac above churning ocean
Both crews spot a cross-shaped disturbance in the water — whitewater the size of a 737. Hovering 50 ft above: a smooth, white, oblong object roughly 40 feet long. No wings, no exhaust. As Fravor descends, the object mirrors his movements — then accelerates away in under two seconds.
VisualMulti-sensor
1:12 PM · ~2 min later
Object vanishes — reappears 60 miles away
The Tic Tac accelerates from near-hover to beyond visual range in under two seconds. Within moments, Princeton's SPY-1 picks it up again — at the fighters' CAP point, 60 miles away. No sonic boom. Implied speed: ~46,000 mph.
SPY-1Anomaly
2:00 PM · Second flight
FLIR1 infrared video captured
WSO Chad Underwood records 76 seconds of IR video. The footage shows a featureless oblong with no exhaust plume, rotating on its longitudinal axis while maintaining flight.
FLIRSensor
Late 2004 – Early 2005
Hard drives confiscated — data vanishes
Unidentified individuals in plain clothes arrive aboard USS Princeton, collect radar data tapes and FLIR recordings, and depart. No receipts. No chain of custody. The originals have never resurfaced.
Data gap
December 16, 2017
Pentagon confirms AATIP, releases FLIR1
NYT publishes bombshell investigation. Pentagon ran a $22M secret UAP program. DoD releases FLIR1 after 13 years. Luis Elizondo resigns AATIP in protest over secrecy.
DoDOfficial
2019 – 2023
Congressional hearings & public testimony
Fravor testifies under oath. Dietrich on 60 Minutes. Kevin Day speaks publicly. AARO is established. The Nimitz encounter becomes the most cited UAP case in legislative history.

The Witnesses

CDR David Fravor
CO VFA-41 "Black Aces" · Ret.
Observed the Tic Tac at close range. Attempted to intercept. Object mirrored his descent, then accelerated instantaneously. 18 years experience, 3,500+ flight hours, Top Gun graduate.
“It accelerated like nothing I've ever seen.”— JRE #1361, 2019
AD
LCDR Alex Dietrich
Wingman · VFA-41
Observed from higher altitude. Independently corroborated all details — ocean disturbance, mirroring behavior, instantaneous departure. First female fighter pilot to speak publicly.
“I don't identify it as any kind of aircraft that I know.”— 60 Minutes, May 2021
KD
Sr. Chief Kevin Day
USS Princeton CIC · Radar Operator
First to identify anomalous radar returns. Tracked objects over two weeks. Personally vectored Fravor's flight. Later reported cognitive effects post-encounter. 20+ years Navy, Top Secret clearance.
