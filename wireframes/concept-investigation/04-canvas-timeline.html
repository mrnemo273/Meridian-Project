The Tic Tac
Incident
USS Nimitz Carrier Strike Group
Pacific Ocean, November 2004
During pre-deployment exercises off San Diego, the USS Nimitz CSG encountered multiple anomalous aerial vehicles over five days. The primary encounter involved visual contact by two F/A-18F crews, AN/SPY-1B radar tracking, and infrared video capture. The object — white, featureless, approximately 40 feet long — demonstrated flight characteristics far beyond any known technology.

USS Princeton's AN/SPY-1B radar detects objects at 80,000+ ft, descending to sea level in seconds — rates exceeding ballistic missile profiles. Visual contact — the Tic Tac above churning ocean

The Intercept
At altitude, both crews spot a cross-shaped disturbance in the water — whitewater the size of a 737. Hovering 50 ft above it: a smooth, white, oblong object roughly 40 feet long. No wings, no exhaust, no visible propulsion. As Fravor descends, the object mirrors his movements — then accelerates away in under two seconds.

The Tic Tac accelerates from a near-hover to beyond visual range in less than two seconds. Within moments, Princeton's SPY-1 picks it up again — at the fighters' CAP point, 60 miles away. No sonic boom. No deceleration signature. The transit implies speeds exceeding 46,000 mph.

Second flight
A second flight launches. WSO Chad Underwood acquires the object on the F/A-18's ATFLIR pod and records 76 seconds of infrared video. The footage shows a featureless oblong with no exhaust plume, no control surfaces, and no visible propulsion — rotating on its longitudinal axis while maintaining flight. The object eventually accelerates off-screen left.

Sensor Data SPY-1
60-mile instant relocation
Object reappears at the fighters' Combat Air Patrol point — a pre-assigned coordinate known only to Navy personnel. No deceleration observed. No sonic boom detected by any asset. Multiple Princeton CIC operators confirmed the track in real time. The CAP point knowledge implies the object had awareness of Navy operations.
Implied speed: ~46,000 mph / Mach 60
Confirmed by: Princeton radar, Kevin Day
Physical Evidence Video
FLIR1 footage analysis
76 seconds recorded, only 35 seconds publicly released. Infrared imaging shows zero exhaust plume — thermodynamically impossible for any known jet or rocket propulsion. Object rotates on its longitudinal axis while maintaining trajectory. SCU analysis estimates acceleration of 75–5,000g. The missing 41 seconds remain classified.
Analysis: SCU, TTSA, multiple physicists
Resolution: Downgraded copy (original confiscated)

Cross-shaped roiling whitewater roughly the size of a Boeing 737. No submarine or surface vessel detected in the area by Princeton's sensors. Disturbance ceased simultaneously with the Tic Tac's departure. Suggests possible subsurface component or interaction with the water column.

Original SPY-1 data tapes, full FLIR recordings, and radar logs. Two crew members (PO Hughes & PO Voorhis) describe men in plain clothes arriving by helicopter. Chain of custody entirely unknown. Only a lower-resolution copy of FLIR1 survived.

FLIR1 officially declassified. DoD confirms $22M Advanced Aerospace Threat Identification Program budget (2007–2012). 38 research papers commissioned on exotic propulsion and spacetime metrics.

Published March 2024. Nimitz cited as among the most significant unresolved cases. Report concludes "no evidence of extraterrestrial activity" — but the classified annex remains sealed and inaccessible to Congress.

Acceleration analysis: 75g – 12,600g
SCU estimates 75–5,000g based on FLIR1 tracking data. Alternative calculation using radar timestamps yields ~12,600g. Human tolerance: 9g. F-35 structural limit: 13.5g.
