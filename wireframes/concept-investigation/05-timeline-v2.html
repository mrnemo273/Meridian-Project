The Tic Tac Incident
USS Nimitz Carrier Strike Group, Pacific Ocean, November 2004
During pre-deployment exercises off San Diego, the USS Nimitz CSG encountered multiple anomalous aerial vehicles over five days. The primary encounter involved visual contact by two F/A-18F crews, AN/SPY-1B radar tracking, and infrared video capture. The object — white, featureless, approximately 40 feet long — demonstrated flight characteristics far beyond any known technology.
Case 1 · Tier 1 · 5 Channel Corroboration

USS Princeton's AN/SPY-1B radar detects objects at 80,000+ ft, descending to sea level in seconds — rates exceeding ballistic missile profiles. Senior Chief Kevin Day tracks multiple returns over two weeks. Objects appear in clusters, linger, then vanish.
SPY-1Radar
November 14, 2004
Fravor & Dietrich vectored to contact
1:00 PM Pacific Standard Time
CDR David Fravor and LCDR Alex Dietrich, flying two F/A-18F Super Hornets from VFA-41 "Black Aces," are diverted from a training exercise. USS Princeton's combat information center vectors them to a live radar return approximately 100 miles southwest of San Diego.

November 14, 2004
Visual contact — the Tic Tac above churning ocean
1:10 PM · The Intercept
At altitude, both crews spot a cross-shaped disturbance in the water — whitewater the size of a 737. Hovering 50 ft above it: a smooth, white, oblong object roughly 40 feet long. No wings, no exhaust, no visible propulsion. As Fravor descends, the object mirrors his movements.
FravorDietrich
November 14, 2004
Object vanishes — reappears 60 miles away
1:12 PM · ~2 min later
The Tic Tac accelerates from a near-hover to beyond visual range in less than two seconds. Within moments, Princeton's SPY-1 picks it up again — at the fighters' CAP point, 60 miles away. No sonic boom. The transit implies speeds exceeding 46,000 mph.
SPY-1Sensor
November 14, 2004
FLIR1 infrared video captured
2:00 PM · Second flight
WSO Chad Underwood acquires the object on the F/A-18's ATFLIR pod and records 76 seconds of infrared video. The footage shows a featureless oblong with no exhaust plume, no control surfaces — rotating on its longitudinal axis while maintaining flight.
FLIRSensor
Late 2004 – Early 2005
Hard drives confiscated — data vanishes
Date unrecorded
Unidentified individuals in plain clothes arrive aboard USS Princeton, collect the radar data tapes and FLIR recordings, and depart. Multiple crew members corroborate this independently. No receipts. No chain of custody. The originals have never resurfaced.

After 20 years, the encounter remains unexplained. Five-channel sensor corroboration, confiscated data, and flight characteristics exceeding known technology by orders of magnitude. No conventional explanation has survived scrutiny.
