The USS Nimitz Encounter

In November 2004, U.S. Navy pilots from the USS Nimitz carrier strike group encountered a white, oblong, featureless object — now known as the "Tic Tac" — during routine training exercises off the coast of San Diego.

01 · The Story

What Happened

On the afternoon of November 14, 2004, Commander David Fravor and Lieutenant Commander Alex Dietrich were flying F/A-18F Super Hornets on a routine training mission approximately 100 miles southwest of San Diego. They were vectored by the USS Princeton, which had been tracking anomalous radar contacts for the previous two weeks — objects that appeared at 80,000 feet, then dropped to 20,000 feet in a matter of seconds.

When Fravor and Dietrich arrived at the coordinates, they observed a disturbance in the ocean below — a churning, cross-shaped area of whitewater roughly the size of a 737. Above this disturbance, a white, oblong object approximately 40 feet long hovered erratically, moving rapidly side to side and up and down, "like a ping pong ball bouncing off a wall."

"It accelerated like nothing I've ever seen. I was a pilot for eighteen years at that point. I'd seen a lot of stuff. This was beyond anything." Commander David Fravor, USN (Ret.)

As Fravor descended to investigate, the object appeared to mirror his movements — ascending to meet him as he spiraled down. When he attempted to close distance, the object accelerated away in less than two seconds, disappearing from visual range. It was next detected by the Princeton's radar approximately 60 miles away — having covered that distance almost instantaneously.

A second flight launched shortly after. Lieutenant Chad Underwood, flying a Super Hornet equipped with an ATFLIR targeting pod, captured the now-famous infrared video showing the object — later released as the "Tic Tac" video. The object exhibited no visible flight surfaces, exhaust plume, or propulsion mechanism.

Why This Case Matters

The Nimitz encounter is widely considered the most significant UAP case of the 21st century. It combines multiple independent sensor systems (radar, infrared, visual observation from trained military aviators), official government acknowledgment (the Department of Defense confirmed the video's authenticity in 2017), and sworn congressional testimony.

The case directly contributed to the establishment of the UAP Task Force and subsequent AARO investigations. Commander Fravor's testimony before the House Oversight Committee in July 2023 brought the account into the official congressional record under oath.

What makes this case exceptional

Multiple independent sensor confirmations (SPY-1 radar, ATFLIR infrared, visual), credentialed military witnesses with no incentive to fabricate, official DoD video authentication, and sworn congressional testimony. The object demonstrated flight characteristics — including instantaneous acceleration, hypersonic speed without a sonic boom, and apparent transmedium capability — that exceed known human technology as assessed by the witnesses and subsequent investigators.
